You are being asked to join a research study. Participation in this study is voluntary. Even if you decide to join now, you can change your mind later.


1. Research Summary (Key Information):

The information in this section is intended to be an introduction to the study only. Details of the study are listed in the sections below. You can ask questions about the study now and at any time in the future, by contacting us at honeylabonline+experiments@gmail.com.

The purpose of this study is to examine how memory and perceptual functions interact in the brain. We are interested how the brain enables us to perceive streams of information over time. Participation is this study will typically take 15-50 minutes. Participants are required to attend and respond to a variety of stimuli, including sequences of spoken language, voiced syllables, and visual cues. There are no known physical or psychological risks associated with your participation in this study, other than the possibility of fatigue, boredom, or slight discomfort. The only cost for participation is a participant’s time. The benefit of participating in this study is to be involved in the pursuit of knowledge and understanding of the human brain. Participants are compensated at a flat rate or via course credit.


2. Why is this research being done?

This research is being done to examine how memory and perceptual functions interact in the brain. In particular, we are interested how the brain enables us to perceive streams of information over time. People between the ages of 18-65 who speak English may join.


3. What will happen if you join this study?

If you agree to be in this study, we will ask you to do the following things:

You will participate in a computerized task. You will see a series of images or short movies on the screen or may hear a series of sounds or words. Your task is to carefully examine each image or movie, or to carefully listen to each sequence of sounds, so that you can report what you saw or heard. Your reading or listening may be interrupted by alternate tasks, such as having to wait for 15 seconds, simple counting of geometric shapes, or answering comprehension questions about a simple situation.

Sometimes we will ask you to directly report what you saw or heard, for example by providing a yes/no response (e.g. "Was the image moving?" or "Did you hear the same sound twice in a row?"). In other cases, we may ask you to make a judgment about images (e.g. "Which images belong together?") or about other features of the stimuli (e.g. "Which sound was louder?"). In still other cases, we may ask you to make a prediction about the stimuli (e.g. “What would you expect to hear or see next?”).

Alternatively, you may be tested on your memory of what you saw or heard or asked to provide short descriptions of life events. You may also be asked to answer questions about your ability to pay attention and focus. After completing the main experiment, we will ask you to fill out a brief questionnaire that asks about some optional personal information (e.g., your age-range) and gives you an opportunity to comment on your experience performing the task. The study will typically take 20-50 minutes to complete. The first phase of the experiment will typically take 10-30 minutes. The second and third phase of the experiment will each take 5-10 minutes.

All participants will be exposed to all phases of the experiment but the order in which participants see each phase may differ. The ordering of phases seen by each participant are chosen at random, like a coin flip. Additionally, participants may see different stimuli, and the order and selection of these stimuli is chosen at random, like drawing numbers from a hat. At the end of the experiment, you will be asked to complete some brief surveys about the study and your experience.


How long will you be in the study?

You will be in this study for 30-50 minutes.


4. What are the risks or discomforts of the study?

You may get tired or bored when we are asking you questions, or when you are completing questionnaires. There is a risk when answering these questions that information about you may become known to people outside this study. While personally identifiable information is not collected in this study, we do collect demographics information including but not limited to, race, ethnicity, gender, handedness, and age of language acquisition. Please note that all demographic questions are optional and are not required to successfully participate in this study. We also collect information with an attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) questionnaire. It has not been administered for the purpose of diagnosis, but only to measure the variations in how people report their experience with sustained attention. If you feel that your survey responses are indicative of a problem with attention, and would like to explore this issue further, basic information about ADHD can be found at WebMD (https://www.webmd.com/add-adhd/adhd-ways-to-tell) and via contacting your health professionals. Information from online studies will be de- coupled from online IDs to decrease the risk of linking this information with your person. This information will be shared with the NIH’s NDA and other databases, but only after de-identification.

Aside from this, the risks associated with participation in this study are no greater than those encountered in daily life or during the performance of routine physical or psychological examinations or tests.


5. Are there benefits to being in the study?

There is no direct benefit to you from being in this study. This study may benefit society if the results lead to a better understanding of how people process streams of information.

10. Why might we take you out of the study early?

You may be taken out of the study if:

- Staying in the study would be harmful.

- You fail to follow instructions.

- The study is cancelled.

- There may be other reasons to take you out of the study that we do not know at this time.

If you are taken out of the study early, Johns Hopkins may use or give out your information that it has already collected if the information is needed for this study or any follow-up activities.


11. How will the confidentiality of your biospecimens and/or data be protected?

TAny study records that identify you will be kept confidential to the extent possible by law. The records from your participation may be reviewed by people responsible for making sure that research is done properly, including members of the Johns Hopkins University Homewood Institutional Review Board and officials from government agencies such as the National Institutes of Health and the Office for Human Research Protections (all of these people are required to keep your identity confidential). Otherwise, records that identify you will be available only to people working on the study, unless you give permission for other people to see the records. The experimental data and questionnaire responses collected during your participation will be anonymized.

De-identified and anonymized data (e.g. response times, key presses, and questionnaire responses) may be made freely available to the scientific community to encourage scientific openness and reproducibility. This sharing of anonymous data may include depositing data in open data repositories, such as https://openneuro.org, or in curated data repositories, such as the National Institute of Mental Health Data Archive (NDA). If you would like more information about NDA, this is available online at https://nda.nih.gov.

Under some experimental conditions, the anonymized and de-identified raw data, such as generated word associations, may also be shared with other participants. In such cases, the raw data will be manually scrubbed of any personally identifiable information. If you have any concerns about sharing of anonymized data, please do not participate.
